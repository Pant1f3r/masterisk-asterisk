What These Tools Are — and What They Aren't

The Bias Skimmer audits datasets, not companies. It runs against actual rows of decision data — applications, outcomes, features — that you already possess. It cannot evaluate a company's algorithm by typing in the company's name, because companies do not publish their training data or decision logs. The data needed for that kind of audit is private until a regulator (CFPB, EEOC, HUD) or a court orders its disclosure during enforcement or litigation. For publicly available datasets like HMDA mortgage data, the Skimmer works directly.

The Assessment Wizard helps individuals navigate their own situation. It is not legal advice. It does not declare that you were discriminated against. It identifies red flags in your specific denial, names the federal statutes that may apply, points you to the correct enforcement agency, and generates pre-filled letters demanding the explanation you are legally entitled to. The work of the law is done by your complaint, by the regulator's investigation, and by the attorney you may need to consult — not by this tool.

No tool — not this one, not any tool in the field — is 100% accurate. Statistical audits have false positives and false negatives by mathematical necessity. The Skimmer's v1.0.1 limitations are documented honestly in Chapter 13.7 of the book and in the source code itself: Pearson correlation is linear-only, there is no significance testing yet, no domain-specific calibration, and no protected-class imputation. These limitations are roadmap items for v1.1, not hidden defects. Use the Skimmer as a fast first-pass screen, not as a finishing certificate.

The book and tools exist because the duty to audit belongs to the developer first, and the right to demand audit belongs to everyone else. The framework is open. The code is open. The letter templates are open. Take what is useful, build on what is missing, and tell us what you learn.

What's Inside the Book

6 Parts 27 Chapters 7 Appendices 187 Pages 70,000 Words

Part I builds the vocabulary of algorithmic harm. Part II reads seven landmark cases: Gender Shades, COMPAS, Amazon's hiring tool, the Optum healthcare algorithm, Apple Card, the tenant-screening case of Mary Louis, and predictive policing. Part III is the technical core — the four-audit framework, the Bias Skimmer source line by line, an end-to-end worked audit, and how to read results. Part IV is the legal reference: federal statutes, enforcement agencies, state and local law, international frameworks, civil litigation strategy. Part V is the practitioner handbook for developers, procurement officers, affected individuals, and policymakers. Part VI synthesizes — why "bias-free AI" is the wrong goal, the civil rights frame, the forward compact.

About the Author and Imprint

Edward Craig Callender is a software developer, an independent publisher, and the founder of KR0M3D1A©®™ Digital ARC, an imprint focused on the intersection of civil rights, algorithmic systems, and public-interest technology. The Materisk Asterisk is his first book.

Edward writes from outside the academy and outside the major tech companies — from the position of a working developer who builds the tools he describes. The Bias Skimmer, the four-audit framework, and the case-by-case readings in this book were assembled across years of self-directed study, not as theoretical exercises but as the practical instruments he wished had existed when he first began trying to understand how automated systems were making decisions about real people.

A family man, Edward has shared his life with the same partner — the mother of his child — for nearly twenty-six years. He is a registered Republican who believes the civil rights frame in this book belongs to no single political tradition; the protections of Title VII, the Fair Housing Act, and the Equal Credit Opportunity Act were enacted across decades and across both parties, and the algorithmic civil rights framework this book argues for is a continuation of that bipartisan inheritance.

This is his life's work to date. He hopes it is read by the people who can use it: the developer rewriting a flawed model, the attorney filing a complaint, the procurement officer asking a vendor harder questions, the person at home holding an adverse-action notice and wondering whether the law has anything to say.
