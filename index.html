Section I — Preamble

We Declare a State of Digital Emergency

The algorithms that govern our lives were not built for all of us. They were built on data collected in a world shaped by centuries of racial inequality, and they perpetuate that inequality at a scale and speed no human gatekeeper ever could. From credit scores to hiring tools to healthcare algorithms — the code carries the prejudice.

This manifesto is a demand. A demand that the systems governing opportunity, justice, and access be rebuilt with racial equity guardrails, neurodiversity inclusion, and algorithmic accountability at their foundation. A demand that the technology companies who profit from biased systems be financially punished so severely that deploying an unaudited algorithm becomes an existential business risk.

"An algorithm trained on a world built by racism will automate that racism — unless we intervene. Silence is code for complicity. Negligence is grounds for penalty."

We speak to the digital architects, the policymakers, the corporate boards, and the communities harmed. The language of technology is not neutral. When we call backgrounds "white" and foregrounds "black," when we train models on data from redlined zip codes, when we build facial recognition that fails on dark skin — we encode a hierarchy. This manifesto names it, measures it, prices it, and demands its dismantling.

Section II — The Categorization Crisis

Stop Calling Us Colors. We Are Nations.

The most fundamental failure of algorithmic systems begins with how they categorize human beings. When a system labels a person "Black" or "White," it reduces millennia of culture, heritage, and identity to a color on a spectrum — the same spectrum the machine uses to process pixels, where white = 255 (maximum signal) and black = 0 (absence of signal). The machine literally equates lighter values with more data, more presence, more value — and darker values with void.

We demand a new schema. Not color. Heritage. A person of Nigerian descent in Houston is not "Black." They are African American — or Nigerian American. A person of German descent in Milwaukee is not "White." They are European American. This is not political correctness. This is computational accuracy.

Section IV — The Physics of Erasure

How the Machine Reads "White" as Signal and "Black" as Noise

In every imaging system, every sensor, every camera, and every ML model that processes visual data: lighter = more signal, darker = less signal. An IR soap dispenser sends light and waits for reflection. Darker skin absorbs more. The sensor never triggers. The machine decides: nothing is there.

Section V — The Receipts

11 Documented Instances of Algorithmic Racism

Not hypotheticals. Recorded, documented, verified incidents where technology caused direct harm to human beings.
Autonomous Vehicles — 2019-2023 • Georgia Tech / King's College London

Self-Driving Cars Cannot See Dark-Skinned Pedestrians

Georgia Tech tested 8 AI pedestrian detection systems: consistent 5% decrease for darker skin. King's College confirmed 7.5% gap, rising to ~10% at night. Training data had 3.5× more light-skinned pedestrians. UK Law Commission warned vehicles may "struggle to recognize dark-skinned faces in the dark."

Developer Response: No manufacturer has disclosed detection accuracy by skin tone. Researchers: their models are "built upon the same open-source systems." The silence continues.
Consumer Products — 2015-Present • Dragon Con / Chukwuemeka Afigbo

Soap Dispensers, Faucets & Paper Towels Ignore Dark Skin

T.J. Fitzpatrick at Dragon Con: 10/10 dispensers failed on his hands, worked for his white friend. Viral video by Facebook's Afigbo showed dispenser only working when white paper towel placed under it. IR sensors rely on light reflection — darker skin absorbs more light.

Developer Response: Manufacturer Technical Concepts made no public statement. Afigbo: "If you have ever had a problem grasping the importance of diversity in tech, watch this video."
Image Recognition — 2015-Present • Google Photos

Google Photos Labels African Americans as "Gorillas"

Developer Jacky Alciné found Google Photos auto-labeled him and his African American friend as "gorillas." Google blocked the words "gorilla," "chimpanzee," and "monkey" from the system. 8+ years later, Google Photos still cannot identify actual gorillas. The problem was silenced, not solved. Apple copied the same approach.

Developer Response: Censorship instead of correction. Alciné: "I understand HOW this happens; the problem is moreso on the WHY."
Gaming — 2010 • Microsoft Xbox Kinect

Xbox Kinect Cannot See Black Gamers

Kinect infrared depth sensors failed to recognize dark-skinned faces. System couldn't track facial features in low-light — the exact conditions most people game in.

Developer Response: "A tricky light sensor." No recall. Product eventually discontinued.
Consumer Electronics — 2009 • Hewlett-Packard

HP Webcam Cannot Track Black Faces

Viral YouTube video: HP facial recognition webcam tracked a white woman perfectly, completely ignored a Black man beside her.

Developer Response: "Working to improve the technology." No evidence of redesign before sale.
Healthcare — COVID-19 Pandemic

Pulse Oximeters Give Dangerously Inaccurate Readings on Dark Skin

Devices systematically overestimate oxygen levels in dark-skinned patients. During COVID-19: delayed treatment, denied supplemental oxygen, preventable deaths.

Developer Response: FDA issued advisory only after widespread deaths. No mandatory redesign ordered.
Voice Recognition — PNAS 2020 Study

Siri, Alexa & All Major Voice AI — Double Error Rates for Black Speakers

All 5 major speech recognition programs (Apple, Amazon, Google, IBM, Microsoft) showed significant racial disparities. Error rates for Black speakers nearly double those for white speakers.

Developer Response: No company has published racial parity benchmarks for voice assistants.
Social Media — 2020 • Twitter

Image Cropping Algorithm Favors White Faces

Twitter's auto-crop consistently chose white faces over Black faces in the same photo. Confirmed across hundreds of image pairs.

Developer Response: Scrapped the tool. Underlying saliency model bias never publicly explained.
Healthcare — Science, 2019 • Optum

Healthcare Algorithm Denies Care to Millions of African American Patients

Algorithm used healthcare costs as proxy for needs. Less money spent on African American patients historically → algorithm concluded they were healthier. Fixing the bias: African American patients receiving extra care would increase from 17.7% to 46.5%. More than half were denied.

Developer Response: Corrected version developed — after years of the biased version affecting millions across major health systems.
Advertising — 2019 • Facebook / Northeastern University

Facebook Ad Delivery Excludes Minorities from Housing & Job Ads

Algorithm discriminated by race and gender even with neutral targeting. Housing ads shown disproportionately to white users. ProPublica confirmed advertisers could explicitly exclude by race.

Developer Response: "Special Ad Category" introduced. Algorithmic delivery bias — Facebook's own ad optimization — remained unaudited.
Employment — 2018 • Amazon

Amazon Scraps Biased AI Hiring Tool

AI recruiting tool systematically downgraded resumes containing words associated with women. Trained on 10 years of resumes — 89% from men. Tool penalized the word "women's" and graduates of all-women's colleges.

Developer Response: Tool abandoned. Damage already done. No public accounting of how many qualified women were filtered out.
Section VI — The Silence

Why Developers Are Not Stepping Up

Across every documented failure, a pattern: the silence. Developers, engineers, product teams, and corporations consistently refuse accountability.

The Four-Step Algorithm of Corporate Evasion

Step 1: Deny. Frame as an isolated "glitch" rather than systemic design failure. Google called the gorilla incident "appalling" — as if their product acted against their wishes, rather than exactly as trained.

Step 2: Deflect. Blame "the data" or "the algorithm" as if these are autonomous entities rather than products of human decisions. "Unintended bias" — as though neglecting diverse test subjects is an accident rather than a choice.

Step 3: Patch, Don't Fix. Google censored "gorilla." Twitter removed auto-crop. FDA issued an advisory. Every response treats the symptom while preserving the disease.

Step 4: Move On. No executive loses their job. No product team is restructured. No mandatory diversity audit. News cycle ends. Biased products remain deployed at scale.

"Having a dark-skinned person on the team would have resulted in a better product." — Chukwuemeka Afigbo, Facebook

The companies building these systems are not finding the problems themselves, because they are not looking. External accountability — from journalists, academics, and affected communities — remains the primary method of discovery. This is why financial penalties must be so severe that self-auditing becomes cheaper than litigation.

Section VIII — Fair Housing Under Siege

The Fair Housing Act in the Age of Algorithms

The Fair Housing Act of 1968 was written to dismantle overt discrimination. In 2025, discrimination needs only an algorithm that says "high risk." HUD's final rule removed the disparate impact test for most algorithmic tools. Federal fair housing funding was zeroed out. 78 critical grants terminated. Several fair housing organizations closed permanently.

"Bias baked into tenant-screening algorithms is locking out Black, Latino, and immigrant renters, testing whether fair housing laws can withstand political rollbacks in the AI era." — Daily Journal, 2025

The SafeRent class-action in Massachusetts. The Huskey v. State Farm fraud AI case. The Mobley v. Workday employment AI collective action. Courts are beginning to rule that AI systems can be held liable as "agents" of the employer. But enforcement is collapsing as funding is stripped.

Section XII — The Equitable Preferences Doctrine & Interactive Bias Simulator

The Three Pillars of Algorithmic Fairness — And the Proof That They Work

Every algorithm that touches a human life makes a choice: what to look at, what to ignore, and what to explain. The current systems get all three wrong. They look at proxies for race. They ignore the context that makes those proxies discriminatory. And they explain nothing — delivering decisions as opaque verdicts with no recourse, no transparency, and no appeal.

This manifesto mandates a different architecture. Not as a suggestion. Not as a best practice. As a binding requirement for any AI system that makes decisions affecting human lives. The requirement is built on three pillars:

PILLAR 1
WHAT THE ALGORITHM LOOKS AT — Relevant Criteria Only
PILLAR 2
WHAT THE ALGORITHM IGNORES — Proxy Variables Excluded
PILLAR 3
WHAT THE ALGORITHM EXPLAINS — Transparent, Challengeable, Appealable

Pillar 1 — What the Algorithm LOOKS AT

The equitable preference is simple: evaluate people on what is relevant to the decision being made. Nothing more. Nothing less.

For housing: Can you afford the rent? That means income-to-rent ratio, verified payment history, and employment stability. If a government voucher covers 73% of the rent, that is guaranteed income and the algorithm MUST count it. If it does not — as SafeRent's algorithm did not, leading to a $2.275 million settlement — the exclusion of that income is itself the discriminatory act.

For employment: Can you do the job? That means verified skills, certifications, years of relevant experience, and demonstrated competencies. A name does not predict job performance. A zip code does not predict job performance. A school name does not predict job performance — it predicts parental wealth. The University of Washington proved in 2024 that AI prefers white-associated names 85% of the time. That is not a skills assessment. It is a heritage filter.

For healthcare: Are you sick? That means lab results, vital signs, diagnosed conditions, symptoms, and family medical history. Healthcare spending does not measure health — it measures access to healthcare. When Obermeyer et al. proved in Science (2019) that replacing the cost proxy with clinical indicators would increase African American patients flagged from 17.7% to 46.5%, they proved that the algorithm was not measuring health. It was measuring wealth.

For lending: Can you repay? That means debt-to-income ratio, credit payment patterns, and down payment. Medical debt — which disproportionately burdens minority communities due to healthcare access disparities — must be excluded from credit evaluation. Collections under $500 must be excluded. These are not measures of financial responsibility. They are measures of systemic disadvantage.

For criminal justice: What did this person do? That means the specific alleged conduct, the evidence supporting it, and the legal standard of proof. A risk assessment score derived from neighborhood arrest rates is not evidence of individual conduct — it is evidence of policing patterns. COMPAS scoring African Americans as high-risk at 2x the rate of white defendants is not predicting recidivism. It is predicting zip code.

Pillar 2 — What the Algorithm IGNORES

The equitable preference demands that the following categories of data be permanently excluded from any algorithm making high-stakes decisions about human lives:

Name: Correlates with race and heritage at r>0.8. A name tells the algorithm nothing about qualification, health, creditworthiness, or criminal conduct. It tells the algorithm everything about heritage — and heritage is a protected characteristic. Names must be masked before processing.

Zip Code: Correlates with race at r>0.5 in most U.S. metropolitan areas. Zip codes are the digital descendants of 1934 HOLC redlining maps. When an algorithm uses zip code to assess risk, it is reproducing 90 years of segregation inside a neural network. Zip codes must be excluded from risk scoring.

School Name: Correlates with parental wealth and race. An algorithm that prefers Ivy League graduates is an algorithm that prefers the children of wealth. Skill verification — not institutional pedigree — is the equitable standard.

Healthcare Spending: Correlates with race via access barriers. Spending measures privilege, not health. Clinical indicators only.

Employment Gaps: Correlate with caregiving (disproportionately women), disability, incarceration (disproportionately African Americans), immigration processing, and military deployment. Penalizing gaps penalizes demographics.

Skin Reflectance: IR sensors, facial recognition, and camera systems that perform worse on dark skin are not experiencing a technical limitation — they are experiencing a training data failure. Multi-spectrum sensing and full Fitzpatrick calibration are the equitable standard.

Communication Style: Eye contact, vocal tone, accent, speech cadence, facial expression — these measure neurotypical social performance, not competence. An AI interview that scores eye contact is an AI interview that discriminates against autistic candidates. These metrics must be excluded.

Any Feature Correlating with a Protected Class at r>0.3: This is the mathematical threshold. If a data feature correlates with race, gender, age, disability, religion, or national origin at a Pearson coefficient above 0.3, it is a proxy variable and must be either removed or debiased using established techniques (adversarial debiasing, counterfactual data augmentation, demographic parity constraints) before the model is deployed.

Pillar 3 — What the Algorithm EXPLAINS

The equitable preference demands that every algorithmic decision affecting a human life must be accompanied by a human-readable explanation of the factors that drove the decision — and the person affected must have the right to challenge, appeal, and override.

Not this: "Your application has been denied." (SafeRent's response to Mary Louis. No explanation. No appeal. No override. Score: 324. Life: disrupted.)

This: "Your application was evaluated on three factors: (1) income-to-rent ratio: 2.8x (meets threshold of 2.5x), (2) payment history: 94% on-time (meets threshold of 90%), (3) employment verification: confirmed stable for 18 months (meets threshold of 12 months). Result: APPROVED. If you believe this evaluation is incorrect, you may appeal to a human reviewer within 14 days at [contact information]. The human reviewer has the authority to override the algorithmic recommendation."

That is transparency. That is accountability. That is the difference between a machine that serves people and a machine that rules them. The algorithm must explain itself in language a non-technical person can understand. The explanation must identify every factor used. The person must have recourse to a human who can override the machine. If the algorithm cannot explain its decision in plain language, the decision must not be made by the algorithm.

"Judge people by what is relevant. Ignore what is inherited. Explain what you decided. These are not innovations. They are the minimum requirements of justice. The fact that they must be stated as demands in a manifesto in 2026 is itself an indictment of an industry that has spent two decades building systems that violate all three."

Section XV — Financial Accountability

How to Punish Tech Giants Severely Enough to Force Change

Apologies don't fix algorithms. PR statements don't retrain models. The only language technocrats understand is financial pain. The penalties must be so severe that deploying an unaudited algorithm becomes an existential business risk — not a calculated cost of doing business.

Penalty Framework: Revenue-Proportional Fines

Tier 1 — Negligent Deployment (No Bias Audit): 4% of global annual revenue per incident. For a company earning $300B, that's $12 billion per unaudited algorithm. Modeled on GDPR's maximum fine structure, but applied specifically to algorithmic discrimination.

Tier 2 — Known Bias, No Correction: 8% of global annual revenue + mandatory injunctive relief. If a company knows its algorithm discriminates and ships it anyway (Google Photos gorilla incident, pulse oximeter inaccuracy), the penalty doubles and the company is court-ordered to fix it within 90 days or face daily penalties of $10M.

Tier 3 — Bias Causing Physical Harm or Death: 15% of global annual revenue + criminal liability for executives who authorized deployment. When an autonomous vehicle's biased pedestrian detection contributes to a death, or a biased pulse oximeter leads to denied treatment and death, this is not a "bug." It is negligent homicide enabled by corporate indifference.

Mandatory Pre-Deployment Requirements

Algorithmic Impact Assessment: Every high-stakes AI system must undergo independent third-party bias audit across all heritage groups and Fitzpatrick skin types BEFORE deployment. Results must be publicly filed.

Demographic Parity Certification: AI training data must demonstrate proportional representation. The 44% of U.S. models missing ethnicity data and the 70-90% trained on just 3 states is grounds for immediate market prohibition.

Annual Re-Audit Mandate: All deployed AI systems audited annually. Results published. Failures trigger automatic Tier 1 penalties.

Whistleblower Protections: Engineers who report internal bias findings receive federal whistleblower protection and 10% of any resulting fine.

THE NETHERLANDS — Government Collapse Over Algorithmic Racism

Rechtbank Den Haag, ECLI:NL:RBDHA:2020:1878 — Feb. 5, 2020 • NCJM et al. v. The State of the Netherlands

SyRI Algorithm Banned — First Court in the World to Halt AI on Human Rights Grounds

The Dutch government's System Risk Indication (SyRI) algorithm cross-referenced personal data from municipalities, UWV (Employee Insurance Agency), SVB (Social Insurance Bank), IND (Immigration and Naturalization Service), and national tax authorities to "predict" welfare fraud. SyRI was deployed exclusively in low-income "problem neighborhoods" with high immigrant populations across 22 projects since 2008. The UN Special Rapporteur on extreme poverty, Professor Philip Alston, filed a third-party intervention warning that SyRI is "the digital equivalent of fraud inspectors knocking on every door in a certain area." The District Court of The Hague ruled SyRI violated Article 8 of the European Convention on Human Rights (right to private life). The court found: "There is in fact a risk that SyRI inadvertently creates links based on bias, such as a lower socio-economic status or an immigration background." SyRI was declared unlawful and immediately halted. The court noted that SyRI breached both the GDPR's purpose limitation and data minimisation principles, and that the government's refusal to disclose its risk model prevented even the court from assessing whether safeguards existed against discrimination. Alston stated: "This judgment will be a wake-up call for politicians, not just in the Netherlands, but in many other countries."

SECTION XXXIV — URGENT

STRUCTURAL FAILURE IN AI ARCHITECTURE — Racial Proxy Disclosure

This is the most dangerous finding in the entire manifesto. The prevailing industry response to algorithmic bias is to remove race from the input data. Delete the race field. Anonymize the name. Strip the zip code. This approach is called "fairness through unawareness" — and it does not work. It has never worked. And the AI industry knows it does not work.

The Proxy Reconstruction Problem

AI systems can reconstruct race from data even when race has been explicitly removed. This is not a theory. It is a verified, replicated, peer-reviewed finding:

MIT / Harvard (2022): Researchers found that AI can accurately predict patient self-reported race from medical images alone — chest X-rays, cardiac ultrasounds, mammograms — even though there are no known human-readable race correlates in these images. Even when images were degraded past the point of being recognizable as medical images, the deep learning models still predicted race with high accuracy. The researchers could not identify what the AI was detecting. The lead researcher stated: "Even when you filter medical images past where the images are recognizable as medical images at all, deep models maintain a very high performance." Human experts could not predict race from the same images. The AI could.

PMC / Adversarial Autoencoders (2024): A study confirmed that race is "highly correlated with other input variables" and that "models that do not include race explicitly as an input variable might still rely on proxies of race, thereby implicitly relying on a high-fidelity estimate of race." Even when researchers used adversarial training specifically designed to prevent the latent space from encoding race, residual correlations persisted. Standard models without adversarial debiasing reconstructed race with high fidelity from supposedly race-neutral variables.

UK Information Commissioner's Office (ICO): The UK's data protection authority confirmed that "removing particular attributes to mitigate the risk of discrimination will not necessarily achieve the intended outcome" because "a variety of features in the training data are often closely correlated with protected characteristics in non-obvious ways" — and advanced machine learning is specifically designed to detect such correlations, including "complex combinations of features which might be correlated with protected characteristics in non-obvious ways."

BCG GAMMA / Proxy Detection Research: Researchers demonstrated that "many AI systems that do not use any demographic features such as gender or race can still produce results that show clear discrimination" because "proxy variables — one or more variables that encode the protected attribute with a substantial degree of accuracy" persist throughout the feature space. Even when individual features show weak correlation with race, combinations of multiple features reconstruct race with high accuracy.

University of Milan / Constitutional Law Analysis: Legal scholar Costanza Nardocci documented that proxy discrimination occurs "every time the proxy perpetuates biases by disproportionately affecting certain individuals and groups, even without grounding the distinction on classical factors of discrimination." The critical finding: "the proxy can be anything" — meaning it "cannot be identified or defined ex-ante." You cannot know in advance which combination of seemingly neutral variables will reconstruct race.

What This Means: The Architecture Is the Bias

Every AI system operating today — every hiring algorithm, every lending model, every healthcare risk scorer, every tenant screening tool, every ad delivery platform, every predictive policing system — uses some form of machine learning that is specifically designed to find patterns in data. When race is correlated with zip code, school attended, name frequency, credit history, healthcare spending, arrest records, employment gaps, housing history, and hundreds of other variables — and it is, because centuries of structural racism produced those correlations — then the AI will find those correlations and use them. Removing the race field does nothing because race is already encoded in everything else.

This is not a bug. This is how the architecture works.

A neural network's entire purpose is to detect patterns humans cannot see. When the pattern it detects is "this combination of zip code + credit score + school + name frequency predicts race with 94% accuracy," and race correlates with the training label (hired/not hired, approved/denied, flagged/not flagged), the model will exploit that pattern. Deleting the race column does not delete the racial signal. It just makes the racial signal invisible to auditors — while remaining perfectly visible to the model.
